Data sources & methodology

This page documents where the data on this site comes from and exactly how it is processed into the maps and charts you see. It is an independent visualization, not an official product of the Ville de Montréal; all measurements are the City's, redistributed here under an open licence. For background on the monitoring network itself — its history, coverage, and why stations measure different things — see the Network page.

Data sources

Three open datasets from the Ville de Montréal portal feed this application. All are fetched directly from their published download URLs; no scraping of restricted paths is involved.

  • Multi-pollutant hourly concentrations — the per-station hourly readings for each substance. RSQA — Multi-Polluants (en continu).
  • Station list — names, addresses, boroughs, and the latitude/longitude used to place the map markers. RSQA — Liste des stations.
  • Air Quality Index (IQA) — the City's pre-computed per-pollutant sub-indices (the détaillé par station resource). RSQA — Indice de la qualité de l'air.

Base map tiles are © OpenStreetMap contributors, styled by CARTO. The RSQA data is © Ville de Montréal, distributed under the Creative Commons CC-BY 4.0 licence. A general overview of the network is on the City site: Monitoring air quality in Montréal.

Scope and cleaning

The build covers the full published archive, 1986–2024 — every annual file the City has released. A preprocessing step ingests all 39 years and turns them into compact files so the browser downloads only what a given view needs: a daily-resolution series per station that powers the map and long-range charts, hourly detail per station-year fetched on demand, and a compact per-year summary of each station's daily cycle that powers hour-of-day profiles over long ranges.

  • Schema drift is absorbed. Across four decades the files vary — the station and timestamp column names change, pollutant spellings differ (PM25, PM2.5, PM2,5), some years split the date and hour into two columns or prepend a station-name column, and a few have a stray leading blank line. The parser detects columns by name rather than position and normalizes spellings to a single catalogue.
  • Timestamps are parsed in either era's format and stored as UTC, then the chart displays Montréal local time. Files up to 2012 label the start of each hour; from 2013 on they label the end (with 24:00 meaning next-day midnight). That ≤1-hour labelling difference is immaterial at the day/month/year scales used for long-range views. The open data is also in Eastern Standard Time year-round (the one-hour summer offset noted on the Network page).
  • Non-numeric cells are excluded. Instrument status flags (down, alarm, InVld, calibration states, communication losses, and N/M / blank for "not measured") are treated as missing rather than as values.
  • The station set and pollutant suite evolve. Stations open and close over the decades and instruments are added or retired (legacy metrics such as the coefficient of haze appear only in early years; black carbon from 2017, ultrafine particles from 2021). The map's year selector shows only the stations and substances that reported in the chosen year, and a few closed historical stations without published coordinates are omitted from the map.

Aggregation

Because these are concentrations rather than counts, values are combined by averaging, not summing.

  • Time series — each Hour / Day / Week / Month bucket shows the mean of the hourly readings that fall in it. Partial leading/trailing Week and Month buckets are trimmed so the line never dips on a half-formed period, and gaps longer than one bucket break the line rather than interpolating across missing data.
  • Map — each station's marker and the interpolated surface use the mean, median, maximum, or minimum over the whole year, whichever you select. The median is robust to the short spikes that pull the mean upward. The legend also reports a station average — the unweighted mean of the per-station values currently shown, so every station counts once regardless of how many hours it reported. Each selectable year is the calendar year of that name — January 1 to December 31 in Montréal local time — aggregated from the City's annual file for that year. The only boundary subtlety is the post-2012 end-of-hour labelling noted above: each year's final hour is timestamped 00:00 on January 1 of the following year, a single midnight reading with no material effect at this scale.
  • Averaging profiles — the time series can fold the selected range onto a short repeating base. Weekday and Weekend give the mean 24-hour diurnal cycle — computed from exact hourly data for ranges up to three calendar years, and from a precomputed per-year summary beyond that, so the profile always reflects the whole selected range; Weekly gives the mean for each day of the week (Mon–Sun) and Annual the mean for each calendar month (Jan–Dec), both from the daily tier over the whole range. These reveal commute-hour and weekday/weekend patterns at traffic-influenced sites, and seasonal cycles such as winter heating or summer ozone.

The Air Quality Index (IQA)

The IQA shown here is the City's official index, not a re-derivation: the source file provides each pollutant's sub-index per station-hour, and the station's index is the maximum across pollutants for that hour — the official definition. No unit conversion or reference constants are introduced.

Higher means worse. The index runs on three acceptability bands: 0–25 good, 26–50 acceptable, and above 50 poor. On the map the IQA is coloured on this absolute scale (green → yellow → red) rather than the relative scale used for raw concentrations, so a colour means the same thing at every station and statistic; the legend shows the bands. On the time-series chart, dashed reference lines mark the 25 and 50 thresholds.

Each map marker also names the driving pollutant: under the Maximum statistic, the pollutant responsible for that station's single worst hour; otherwise the pollutant that drove the index in the largest share of hours over the year, with that share. In 2024 ozone drove the index most hours, but the worst peaks were fine-particle (PM2.5) episodes.

The index is a regional health indicator, not a source-attribution tool. Its dominant components — ozone and fine particles — are largely secondary pollutants that form in the atmosphere downwind of their precursors, over hours and many kilometres. The IQA therefore characterizes the broad airshed rather than nearby emissions: it cannot — and was not built to — resolve a local source such as the Décarie corridor. Questions about traffic proximity are better answered by the directly-emitted markers (NO, NO₂, black carbon, ultrafine particles), which is partly why the network deploys those instruments selectively (see the Network page).

One caveat for comparison with official summaries: this app aggregates every metric by bucket mean, so a "daily" IQA here is the mean of the hourly index rather than the daily maximum some agencies report. Use the map's Maximum statistic for a worst-case reading. The City began publishing the index in 2007, so the IQA is available from 2007 onward; earlier years show the measured pollutants only.

The map's interpolated surface

The stations are sparse points, so the coloured overlay is an inverse-distance-weighted (IDW) interpolation: each pixel blends the station values, weighting nearer stations more heavily. To avoid implying certainty where there are no sensors, the overlay's opacity fades with distance from the nearest station and disappears a few kilometres out. A substance measured at only one station (for example benzene) shows that single marker with no surface, since there is nothing to interpolate between.

Notes and limitations

  • This is an unofficial visualization; for authoritative figures consult the City's own publications and the open datasets linked above.
  • Coverage is uneven by design — see the Network page — so cross-station comparisons are only meaningful for substances several stations measure.
  • Long-range comparisons should be read with the network's evolution in mind: changing stations, instruments, and siting over 1986–2024 mean trends reflect both real air-quality change and monitoring change.
