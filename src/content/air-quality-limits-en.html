Air Quality Limits and Guidelines

This page explains the limits and guidelines against which the air quality readings in this app can be compared. The most important thing to understand before reading any number is that there are two different kinds of threshold, and they are not the same: regulatory standards, which a jurisdiction commits to meeting, and health-based guidelines, which describe the levels below which health effects are minimized. The two can differ by a factor of two or more, so a reading can be "within the standard" and still well above the health-based guideline.

How to read a limit

A threshold is meaningless without four pieces of context, all of which are easy to miss.

  • Averaging period. The same pollutant has different limits for different exposure windows — an annual mean, a 24-hour mean, an 8-hour mean, a 1-hour peak. A reading only makes sense compared against the limit for the same averaging period.
  • Statistical form. Many regulatory standards are not a simple average. The Canadian standards, for example, are typically assessed as a three-year average of a specific yearly statistic (the annual mean, or the 98th percentile of daily values). A single year above the number is not automatically an exceedance, and a low single year can mask a higher three-year metric.
  • Units. Gases are reported in this app in parts per billion (ppb); some guidelines are expressed in micrograms per cubic metre (µg/m³). Converting between them depends on temperature and pressure. At 25 °C, approximate conversions are: NO₂ 1 ppb ≈ 1.88 µg/m³, O₃ 1 ppb ≈ 1.96 µg/m³, SO₂ 1 ppb ≈ 2.62 µg/m³, and CO 1 ppm ≈ 1.145 mg/m³. Particulate matter (PM2.5, PM10) is already a mass concentration (µg/m³) and needs no conversion.
  • How achievement is judged. Regulatory achievement is assessed for an air zone using designated reporting stations, and there are mechanisms to set aside data influenced by exceptional events such as wildfire smoke. A near-road station records a localized maximum and is not necessarily the station used to determine whether a region meets a standard.

Canadian Ambient Air Quality Standards (CAAQS)

The CAAQS are the regulatory benchmarks that apply in Canada, set through the Canadian Council of Ministers of the Environment (CCME) and implemented by the provinces. They are explicitly not a "pollute-up-to" level: the CCME and Health Canada state that population health effects occur at concentrations below the standards [1][2]. Some pollutants have staged values that tighten over time; the table below lists the value currently in effect.

The precise metric definitions are specified by the CCME and should be treated as authoritative over the simplified forms above.

World Health Organization (WHO) air quality guidelines, 2021

The WHO guidelines are health-based and advisory, not regulatory. They were substantially tightened in 2021 to reflect evidence that harm occurs at lower concentrations than previously recognized, and they are considerably stricter than most regulatory standards [4]. The WHO is explicit that there is no identified safe threshold for fine particulate matter. The guidelines also add good-practice statements for pollutants with no numeric guideline, including black carbon and ultrafine particles.

United States standards (NAAQS), for comparison

The US national standards are included only as a familiar point of comparison; they do not apply in Canada. The annual fine-particle standard is currently unsettled: the EPA lowered it from 12.0 to 9.0 µg/m³ in February 2024, but in late 2025 the agency moved to have that revision vacated, which would restore the 12.0 µg/m³ level — so as of early 2026 its status is in flux and should be verified [5][6].

Quebec and Montreal: the regulation and the index

In Quebec, ambient air is regulated provincially through the Règlement sur l'assainissement de l'atmosphère (RAA), administered by the environment ministry (MELCCFP), within the national CAAQS framework [7]. On the Island of Montréal, the monitoring network (RSQA) is operated by the city.

The Air Quality Index (Indice de la qualité de l'air, IQA) reported by the network is a derived communication index, not itself a limit. For each measured pollutant a sub-index is computed as the concentration divided by a reference value and scaled so that an index of 50 corresponds to that reference; the overall hourly index is the highest of the pollutant sub-indices. The reported bands are good (0–25), acceptable (26–50), and poor (above 50) [8]. Because the index takes the maximum across pollutants, it is usually governed by whichever pollutant is highest at the time — frequently ozone — and it does not isolate the contribution of any single source.

Interpreting the readings in this app

Two practical points follow from the tables above. First, the gases in this app are in ppb, while the WHO guidelines are in µg/m³, so a conversion is needed to compare them: an annual NO₂ of about 12 ppb, for instance, is roughly 22 µg/m³, which is just inside the current Canadian standard yet more than twice the WHO guideline of 10 µg/m³. Second, a value can be both "within the regulatory standard" and "above the health-based guideline" at the same time, and both statements are true — which is why this page lists both kinds of threshold rather than a single number.

How Montréal compares to other large cities

The readings at the Décarie interchange — the busiest and most pollution-exposed point in the local network — are modest by the standards of large cities worldwide. The table below sets the interchange against representative cities in North America, Europe, and Asia, on two pollutants that behave very differently: fine particulate matter (PM2.5) and nitrogen dioxide (NO₂). A population column is included only to give a rough sense of each city's scale. One caveat governs the whole table: most of these figures are city-wide or urban-background values, whereas the Décarie figure is a single near-road station — a local maximum — so the comparison understates how clean Montréal is relative to these cities' own traffic hotspots.

A note on the nitrogen-oxide column: it reports NO₂, not raw NO. Nitric oxide (NO) is so short-lived that it is not published as a comparable city-level annual figure — it is meaningful only right at the roadside, as in this app's own RSQA data — so NO₂, the regulated traffic marker, is used instead. Values labelled "bg" are urban background; roadside or kerbside figures are noted separately.

The two columns behave differently, which is why both are shown. PM2.5 is largely a regional, background pollutant — carried in from distant sources and wildfire smoke — so it varies little across a city and clusters the cleaner cities (Montréal, Toronto, Ottawa, Boston, Paris, Amsterdam, Copenhagen, London, and Tokyo) within a few µg/m³ of one another. NO₂ is produced locally by traffic and is short-lived, so it varies sharply within a single city: roadside concentrations commonly run several times the urban background, by a factor reported in Paris of one to six [19]. This is why a near-road site can sit close to the city average on PM2.5 yet well above it on NO₂ — visible in the two Montréal rows, where the near-road station matches the city on PM2.5 but reads about 1.6 times the city background on NO₂.

In absolute terms the cities span a wide range. The cleaner ones sit at a few times the WHO PM2.5 guideline of 5 µg/m³, against roughly 6× in Beijing and 18× in Delhi. Being low by global standards is not the same as meeting the health-based guidelines, however: every city in the table exceeds both the WHO PM2.5 and NO₂ guidelines, and the WHO identifies no safe threshold for fine particulates.

Caveats. The NO₂ column is rougher than the PM2.5 column. NO₂ is strongly site-dependent — urban-background and roadside readings in the same city can differ several-fold — so figures are labelled "bg" (urban background) where known, and values marked "approx." are indicative estimates from mixed national networks and years; the best-sourced entries are Paris, London, Copenhagen, and the two Montréal rows. The Montréal figures are computed from this app's own RSQA data (both 2024 station averages), which is why the Montréal PM2.5 differs slightly from official aggregates such as IQAir's 8.9 µg/m³ for 2024; the other cities use their own official or aggregated figures, so cross-city comparison is approximate. Population figures are approximate metropolitan or urban-area values with inconsistent definitions between countries and indicate scale only. North American PM2.5 for 2023–2024 is inflated by wildfire smoke.
