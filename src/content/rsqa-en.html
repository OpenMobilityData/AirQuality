The Montréal Air Quality Monitoring Network (RSQA)

This page describes the Réseau de surveillance de la qualité de l'air (RSQA), the network of fixed stations that measures ambient air pollution across the Island of Montréal. It explains how the network is structured, why its geographic coverage and its set of measured pollutants are uneven, and what is and isn't publicly documented about how it reached its present shape.

This page grew out of a focused interest in air quality along the Autoroute Décarie corridor in Côte-des-Neiges–Notre-Dame-de-Grâce (CDN-NDG) — the city's most populous borough, and unusual in Montréal for its dense residential occupancy reaching right up to the edge of the expressway trench. That motivation shapes some of the emphasis below, especially the gaps in coverage through CDN-NDG and along the Décarie corridor. The network it describes, and the maps and charts this site provides, nonetheless span the whole Island of Montréal and are useful well beyond any single corridor.

A note on certainty: the structure of the network and the logic behind its measurements are well documented in official sources. The reasons specific stations were closed are not publicly documented; where this page discusses closures it distinguishes documented facts from reasonable inference.

The network at a glance

The RSQA is operated by the Ville de Montréal and is the Montréal node of a national program: the National Air Pollution Surveillance (NAPS / SNPA) program, a collaboration established in 1969 between Environment and Climate Change Canada (ECCC) and provincial, territorial, and regional governments. The City has participated for over five decades. Across the wider province, the provincial network (RSQAQ) is run by the Québec environment ministry (MELCCFP), but the stations on the Island of Montréal are the City's responsibility rather than the province's [1][2][7][9].

The network's purpose, under NAPS guidelines, is to produce a long-term record of reliable, defensible, and accessible data, following standard ambient-air-monitoring and quality assurance / quality control practices [2].

As of the 2023 annual report, the network consisted of roughly:

  • 11 permanent sampling stations, plus 2 temporary stations dedicated to special projects;
  • about 60 continuous analyzers and 15 intermittent ("punctual") samplers;
  • a technical team of about 8 people responsible for maintenance, calibration, and data validation.

The network publishes a real-time Air Quality Index (Indice de la qualité de l'air, IQA). The hourly index is the highest of the sub-indices computed for five continuously measured pollutants (carbon monoxide, nitrogen dioxide, ozone, fine particulate matter PM2.5, and sulphur dioxide). An index of 0–25 is reported as "good," 26–50 as "acceptable," and above 50 as "poor" [2][5].

A brief history and how the network evolved

Continuous air monitoring in the region dates to the early 1970s, and the open-data archive carries that long historical component forward; closed stations are retained in the station list specifically so their past data can still be linked [3].

Over those decades the network changed substantially. Several pollutants that originally justified monitoring have fallen dramatically — sulphur dioxide, for example, has declined sharply across Canada since the 1970s — while monitoring priorities shifted toward fine particulates, ozone, and traffic-related pollutants [8]. Instruments are replaced as they reach the end of their useful life under program guidelines; in 2023, for instance, the older Sharp 5030 particulate analyzers were progressively replaced with Teledyne T640 units [2]. The present network is therefore best understood as the accumulated result of many decisions over time, not a single uniform design.

Quirks of geographic coverage

Coverage is uneven, and two features matter for understanding the data for the central-western part of the island.

No station in Côte-des-Neiges–Notre-Dame-de-Grâce

In the open-data station list — including its closed and historical entries — no station is coded to the borough of Côte-des-Neiges–Notre-Dame-de-Grâce (CDN-NDG). On this record the borough has not had its own monitoring station; it was historically sampled only at its edges by stations in the neighbouring enclave municipalities (for example Hampstead and the Town of Mount Royal), and those edge stations are now closed [3]. The accurate framing is therefore not that CDN-NDG lost a station, but that it was never covered from within and its peripheral coverage has lapsed.

The Décarie corridor is monitored only at its northern end

The Autoroute Décarie (A-15) trench runs north–south through the central-western island. The only currently open station on that corridor is station 28, "Échangeur Décarie," located at the A-15/A-40 interchange at the northern end of the trench (administratively in the Town of Mount Royal). It is a genuine traffic-influenced station: the 2023 report attributes the poor-air-quality days at stations 28 and 103 specifically to highway traffic [2]. However, the long stretch of the trench running south through CDN-NDG has no open fixed station for air quality.

Inconsistent pollutant detection across stations

Different stations measure different pollutants. This is by design rather than oversight, for three reasons.

Stations have individual objectives

In a NAPS-style network, each station is sited and instrumented to answer a particular question rather than to provide uniform coverage. The 2023 report makes this explicit by attributing each station's poor-air-quality days to a specific local source — for example, eastern-Montréal industry at station 3, the municipal works yards at station 17, highway traffic at stations 28 and 103, port activity and boulevard traffic at station 50, and winter wood-burning at station 55. A station's instrument suite follows the sources it exists to characterize [2].

Specialized instruments are expensive and were added late

The core index pollutants (NO2, O3, PM2.5, CO, SO2) use standard, robust analyzers and are widely deployed. By contrast, elemental / black carbon and ultrafine-particle counts are measured by costly, maintenance-intensive instruments deployed only at a few characterization sites. In the continuous dataset, black carbon appears only from 2017 onward and ultrafine particles only from 2021 onward. As a result, black carbon and ultrafine particles are available at only a small number of stations (in 2023, both at stations 3 and 17, and ultrafine particles also at station 99) [2][4].

A practical consequence: the busiest near-road site on the Décarie (station 28) measures the standard gas-plus-PM2.5 suite but not black carbon or ultrafine particles — the two measurements that most directly fingerprint traffic exhaust. Those instruments are deployed elsewhere in the network [2].

The network changes over time

Because instruments are added, swapped, and retired across decades as guidelines and priorities evolve, the set of pollutants at any given station reflects when and why that station was last equipped — not a single network-wide standard [2].

Why stations close

Documented: The open data confirms which stations are closed and retains them in the list so their historical data remains linkable, but it does not publish a reason for any individual closure [3].

Inferred (not confirmed by an official source): Closures of older stations in this kind of network are typically driven by a combination of factors — consolidation of a denser historical network into fewer, better-instrumented sites; loss of host-of-convenience locations (legacy stations were often placed at arenas, schools, and municipal buildings that later change use); tightening of siting standards that retires non-conforming legacy probes; and the decline of pollutants that some old stations were originally built to measure. These are plausible mechanisms consistent with how monitoring networks evolved generally; they should not be read as the specific, verified reasons for any particular Montréal closure.

To obtain the actual reasons, two routes are likely to work: contacting the network's open-data team (donneesouvertes@montreal.ca), who can route inquiries to the Service de l'environnement; and reviewing the older annual bilans year by year, which sometimes note station additions and retirements [6].

Working with the data: things to watch for

A few conventions in the open datasets are easy to miss and can distort analysis if overlooked.

  • Time zone: timestamps use Eastern Standard Time year-round and do not shift for daylight saving time. During the summer there is effectively a one-hour offset relative to local clock time [5].
  • Hour-label convention changed in 2013: for files up to 2012 the timestamp marks the start of the averaging hour; from 2013 onward it marks the end of the hour [4].
  • Non-numeric cells are status flags, not data: values such as "down" (instrument stopped for calibration or checks), "alarm" (malfunction), "InVld" (flagged invalid), "<Samp" (fewer than 75% of the minute-level data present, so the hour is not valid), calibration states ("span," "calib," "purge," "zero"), communication or power losses ("No Data," "RS232," "FailPwr"), and "N/M" or blank (not measured). These must be excluded when computing concentrations or data-capture rates [4].
  • Data-capture (uptime) is reported separately: the network publishes per-station, per-pollutant operating rates as accountability reports; a conventional completeness threshold of about 75% is used in such networks to judge whether a period is representative [4].
  • Programmatic access: the open-data portal exposes a CKAN query API (datastore_search and datastore_search_sql against a resource id), which allows pulling a single station or a subset of columns without downloading an entire multi-megabyte year file. Confirm a given resource is datastore-enabled before relying on it [10].

What is documented versus inferred

  • Documented: network structure, NAPS membership, station count, the index method, per-station source characterizations, the pollutants and instruments at each station, the availability dates for black carbon and ultrafine particles, data conventions, and which stations are open or closed.
  • Inferred: the specific reasons individual stations were closed, and the general drivers of historical network consolidation.
